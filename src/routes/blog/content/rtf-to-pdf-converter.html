Understanding RTF and PDF

What is RTF?

RTF (Rich Text Format) is a text-based document format developed by Microsoft in the late 1980s. Unlike plain text files, RTF can store formatting information—fonts, bold, italic, colors, paragraph alignment—while remaining readable by virtually any word processor. The format achieved widespread adoption because it provided a way to exchange documents between incompatible software without losing basic formatting.

RTF's key strength is compatibility. A document created in Microsoft Word can be opened in WordPerfect, LibreOffice, Google Docs, or countless other applications. This made RTF the lingua franca of document exchange in the pre-standardization era. You'll still find RTF files in legacy systems, legal archives, and government databases where long-term accessibility trumps advanced features.

What is PDF?

PDF (Portable Document Format) was developed by Adobe in the early 1990s with a different goal: pixel-perfect document reproduction. Unlike RTF, which describes content that flows and adapts, PDF describes exactly how a document should look—down to precise character positions, page breaks, and image placement. What you see in a PDF viewer is exactly what will print.

PDF has become the de facto standard for document sharing in business, academia, and government. It's built into every major operating system, supported by web browsers, and understood by printers worldwide. When you need to ensure your document looks the same on a smartphone in Tokyo and a desktop in Toronto, PDF delivers.

Why Convert RTF to PDF?

1. Universal Viewing

PDFs open everywhere without requiring specialized software. Every modern web browser can display PDFs. macOS and Windows have built-in PDF viewers. Smartphones handle PDFs natively. When you send someone an RTF file, they might not have software that opens it correctly—or their software might reformat the document unexpectedly. Send a PDF, and it just works.

2. Consistent Appearance

RTF documents can look different depending on the software used to open them and the fonts installed on the system. PDF embeds fonts and locks layout, ensuring your document looks identical on every device. This consistency is crucial for contracts, proposals, reports, and any document where presentation matters.

3. Print-Ready Output

PDF was designed with printing in mind. The format preserves exact page breaks, margins, and layout—what you see on screen is what comes out of the printer. If your RTF document needs to be printed professionally (brochures, manuals, formal letters), converting to PDF first ensures predictable results.

4. Professional Standard

In business communication, PDF conveys professionalism. Job applications, contracts, invoices, proposals, and formal reports are expected in PDF format. Sending an RTF file to a potential client or employer might seem outdated or unprepared. PDF signals that you've finalized the document and it's ready for review.

5. Document Security

While RTF files are fully editable by anyone who opens them, PDFs can be secured against editing, printing, or copying. This doesn't make them completely tamper-proof, but it provides a reasonable level of protection for documents that shouldn't be casually modified.

6. Archival and Compliance

PDF/A, a subset of PDF designed for long-term archiving, is recognized by ISO standards and accepted for legal and regulatory compliance. Organizations that need to preserve documents for years or decades often standardize on PDF/A. Converting your RTF documents to PDF is often the first step toward compliant archival.

What's Preserved in Conversion

A proper RTF to PDF conversion maintains document fidelity:

  • Text content — All text, including special characters and symbols
  • Character formatting — Bold, italic, underline, strikethrough, fonts, sizes, and colors
  • Paragraph formatting — Alignment, indentation, line spacing, and paragraph spacing
  • Tables — Row and column structure with borders and basic styling
  • Images — Embedded graphics in standard formats (JPEG, PNG, BMP)
  • Hyperlinks — Clickable URLs that work in PDF viewers
  • Page layout — Margins, page size, and orientation

What May Differ

Some aspects of RTF documents may translate differently to PDF:

  • Fonts — If the original fonts aren't available, similar fonts are substituted. For best results, use common fonts or embed fonts in the PDF.
  • Complex tables — Extremely complex table layouts may need minor adjustment
  • OLE objects — Embedded application objects may render as static images

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is RTF still used?

RTF persists because of its universal compatibility. It's the lowest-common-denominator format that works across virtually all word processors without compatibility issues. Legacy systems, legal requirements, and cross-platform workflows keep RTF relevant. However, for distribution and archival, PDF has largely superseded RTF.

Is RTF to PDF conversion lossless?

For most documents, conversion is effectively lossless—all text, formatting, and images transfer accurately. Very complex layouts or unusual fonts might have minor differences, but the content and overall appearance are preserved.

Can I convert PDF back to RTF?

Yes, though PDF-to-RTF conversion is inherently imperfect because PDF stores visual appearance while RTF stores editable structure. For editing purposes, you'd lose any PDF-specific features. If you need to edit content, it's better to keep the original RTF file.

Will my RTF's page breaks be preserved?

Yes. Manual page breaks in your RTF document convert to page breaks in the PDF. The resulting PDF will have the same page structure as the original.

Can I password-protect the resulting PDF?

The basic conversion produces an unprotected PDF. If you need password protection or editing restrictions, apply them afterward using PDF software like Adobe Acrobat, Preview (Mac), or free tools like PDFtk.

What if my RTF has embedded objects?

OLE objects (embedded Excel charts, for example) typically render as static images in the PDF. The visual appearance is preserved, but the interactive functionality is lost—which is usually the desired behavior for a finalized document.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

Fonts look different in the PDF

If the RTF uses fonts not available on the conversion system, substitutes are used. For consistent results, use common fonts (Arial, Times New Roman, Calibri) or consider embedding fonts in your documents.

Page breaks in unexpected places

RTF documents created without explicit page breaks rely on the viewing software to determine pagination. The PDF conversion fixes pagination based on the content and margins. If page breaks matter, add explicit page breaks in the original RTF.

Images appear low quality

If images in your RTF were low-resolution originally, they'll remain low-resolution in the PDF. The conversion doesn't degrade image quality, but it can't improve what wasn't there. For high-quality output, ensure source images are high-resolution.

File size is too large

PDFs with many images can become large. After conversion, use PDF optimization tools to reduce file size if needed. Adobe Acrobat, Preview (Mac), and online tools can compress PDFs while maintaining reasonable quality.

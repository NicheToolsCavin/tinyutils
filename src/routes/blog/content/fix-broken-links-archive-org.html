Why use archived versions?

  • Preserve context — References make sense with original content
  • SEO value — Keep outbound link equity
  • User experience — Readers can still access cited sources
  • Citations — Academic and journalistic integrity

How to fix with Wayback

  5. Update your links to point to archived versions

How the Wayback Machine works

The Internet Archive's Wayback Machine crawls and saves web pages. It stores historical snapshots that remain accessible even after the original site disappears.

Archive URLs look like: https://web.archive.org/web/20231015/https://example.com/page

Best practices

  • Choose recent snapshots — Closer to when you originally linked
  • Verify content — Make sure the archived version has the content you need
  • Note the archive — Consider adding "(archived)" to link text
  • Batch processing — Fix multiple broken links at once

Limitations

  • Not everything is archived — Some pages were never crawled
  • Dynamic content — JavaScript-heavy sites may not capture well
  • Login-required pages — Private content isn't archived
  • Recent content — Very new pages may not be captured yet

FAQ

What if there's no archived version?

Remove the link, find an alternative source, or update content to remove the reference.

Can I request a page be archived?

Yes, the Wayback Machine's "Save Page Now" feature lets you capture pages. Use it proactively for important links.

Are archived links permanent?

The Internet Archive aims for permanent access, but it's a nonprofit. For critical references, consider additional preservation methods.

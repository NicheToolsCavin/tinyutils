Want to see what a website looked like years ago? The Wayback Machine is your time machine for the web. Here's how to use it effectively.

TL;DR

  • Visit web.archive.org and enter any URL
  • Browse historical snapshots by date
  • Use TinyUtils Wayback Fixer to find archived versions of broken links

What is the Wayback Machine?

The Wayback Machine is a digital archive of the World Wide Web, maintained by the Internet Archive. It contains over 800 billion web pages captured since 1996.

What you can do with it

Research old website designs

See how sites evolved over time. Great for UX research, competitor analysis, or nostalgia.

Recover lost content

Accidentally deleted a page? The archived version might still exist.

Fix broken links

When a cited source goes offline, link to the archived version to preserve your references.

Verify claims

Check what a website actually said at a specific time. Useful for fact-checking and legal matters.

How to use it

  1. Go to web.archive.org
  2. Enter the URL you want to see
  3. Browse the calendar of available snapshots
  4. Click a date to view that version
  5. Navigate the archived site normally

Pro tips

  • Calendar view — Blue dots mean snapshots exist for that date
  • Direct URL — Use web.archive.org/web/*/example.com to see all captures
  • Save pages — Use "Save Page Now" to archive important pages yourself
  • API access — Developers can query the CDX API for bulk lookups

What it can't do

  • Login-required pages — Private content isn't captured
  • All pages — Some sites block archiving or weren't crawled
  • Dynamic content — JavaScript-heavy sites may not render perfectly
  • Real-time — There's a delay between crawls

FAQ

How often are pages archived?

Popular sites might be captured multiple times per day. Lesser-known sites might have snapshots months apart.

Can I remove my site from the archive?

Site owners can request removal, but it's not automatic. The archive respects robots.txt going forward but doesn't delete past captures by default.

Is the archive legal to use?

For research, journalism, and personal use, yes. Republishing archived content may have copyright implications.
